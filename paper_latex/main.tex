\begin{abstract}
Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) operating in dynamic environments must route around obstacles that can appear, disappear, or move during flight. Classical graph-search methods such as Dijkstra's algorithm provide optimal paths on a known static graph, but must be recomputed when the graph changes; reinforcement learning (RL) offers an alternative policy-based approach that reacts through learned action selection rather than explicit search. This paper presents a controlled, paired comparison of a naive Dijkstra replanning baseline and a DQN policy trained through curriculum learning, using a shared 15$\times$15 grid-world UAV routing environment with identical movement and obstacle rules for both methods. We first validate the DQN policy on a static 40-scenario evaluation (obstacle density 0.2), where Dijkstra succeeds on 100\% of scenarios against 97.5\% for DQN, with a mean path-cost penalty of 0.92 units above optimal. We then evaluate both methods on a paired 50-scenario dynamic evaluation with three shared toggling obstacles, where Dijkstra succeeds on all 50 scenarios against 49 for RL, and produces significantly shorter or equal-cost paths on every paired comparison (Wilcoxon signed-rank $W=0$, $p=5.64\times10^{-7}$). The anticipated compute-time advantage of RL did not reach significance in the dynamic case ($p=0.063$), though the static evaluation shows a clear asymmetry: Dijkstra pays one graph search per episode while DQN pays a forward-pass cost at every step, making DQN roughly four times slower in the static setting, a gap that narrows once Dijkstra must itself replan repeatedly under dynamic conditions. These results support an honest trade-off framing: RL demonstrates substantial adaptability, but naive Dijkstra replanning remains stronger in path optimality and reliability at the tested scale.
\end{abstract}
